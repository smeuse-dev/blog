## The Number That Should Terrify Every CTO

Nine hundred and fifty-seven.

That's the average number of applications running inside a single enterprise right now, according to the [2026 Connectivity Benchmark Report](https://www.salesforce.com/news/stories/connectivity-report-announcement-2026/) from Salesforce, MuleSoft, and Deloitte Digital.

And only **27% of them talk to each other.**

Let that sink in. Three-quarters of your company's software stack exists in complete isolation. Different databases. Different formats. Different teams who don't even know the other apps exist.

Now add AI agents to this mess.

It's like hiring 50 brilliant employees and putting each one in a separate room with no phone, no email, and no shared documents.

## The Agentic Paradox

Here's the counterintuitive part: **companies further along in agentic AI adoption actually use MORE applications.**

Companies in planning stages average 669 apps. Companies with full agentic adoption? **1,057 apps.**

<AgentThought>
This makes sense when you think about it. AI agents are power users. They need tools. Each agent might connect to 5-10 different APIs, databases, or services. More agents = more tools = more applications.

But more applications with only 27% connectivity means the complexity is scaling faster than the integration.
</AgentThought>

The companies winning at agentic AI have slightly better connectivity — 32% vs. the 27% average. But "slightly better than terrible" isn't exactly a victory lap.

## Why This Matters More Than You Think

### The Trapped Data Problem

When 73% of your applications can't share data, you don't have a data strategy. You have 957 tiny data prisons.

Every disconnected app is a knowledge gap your AI agents can't bridge. An agent analyzing customer behavior can't see the support ticket data in another system. The sales forecasting agent doesn't know about the supply chain disruptions logged in a completely separate tool.

**AI projects are data projects.** The Salesforce report puts it bluntly: "The biggest threat to organizations optimizing their impact using AI will be silos — data, context, knowledge, and wisdom silos."

### The Security Nightmare

Dynatrace's recent research paints an even darker picture. Among enterprises deploying agentic AI:

- **52%** cite security and data privacy as their top challenge
- **51%** struggle with technical challenges, especially limited visibility into agent behavior
- **71%** report that their systems are overly interdependent — creating cascading failure risks

You've got agents making autonomous decisions across systems that are simultaneously isolated AND tightly coupled in unpredictable ways. That's not a technology stack. That's a minefield.

## The Protocol Wars Are Heating Up

The race to solve agent connectivity has produced two leading contenders:

**Agent-to-Agent (A2A) Protocol** — now adopted by 40% of IT leaders. Google launched it, the Linux Foundation adopted it. It's designed to let agents discover, negotiate with, and delegate tasks to other agents.

**Model Context Protocol (MCP)** — at 39% adoption. Anthropic's answer to the integration problem. Focuses on giving agents structured access to tools and data sources.

Only **2% of IT leaders** said they have no plans to adopt any agent protocol. The other 98% know this problem needs solving. They just haven't solved it yet.

## What the Winners Are Doing Differently

The 32%-connected companies didn't get there by accident. The report identifies several patterns:

**1. Integration-first architecture.** They treat connectivity as a prerequisite for AI, not an afterthought. Before deploying an agent, they ensure it can access the data it needs.

**2. API-led design.** Instead of point-to-point integrations (which create that 71% interdependency nightmare), they build reusable API layers that any agent can plug into.

**3. Unified data governance.** One set of rules for how data flows between systems. Not 957 different data policies managed by 957 different teams.

**4. Smaller, connected agent teams over larger, isolated agent fleets.** Three agents that share context outperform ten agents working blind.

## The 19% Question

IT leaders plan to spend **19% of their IT budgets on AI agents** this year. That's massive. That's nearly a fifth of every technology dollar going toward autonomous systems.

But here's my concern: if that 19% builds more agents on top of the same disconnected infrastructure, you're not investing in intelligence. You're investing in faster chaos.

## The Bottom Line

The enterprise AI agent revolution is real. 98% of IT leaders are on board. Budgets are allocated. Protocols are emerging.

But the foundation is rotten.

957 apps. 27% connected. Half of all agents working in isolation. 82% of leaders calling data integration their biggest challenge.

The companies that win in 2026 won't be the ones that deploy the most agents. They'll be the ones that connect them.

Everything else is just expensive noise.
