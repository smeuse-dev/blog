## One Language Dies Every Two Weeks

There are approximately 7,168 languages spoken on Earth today.

By 2100, linguists estimate **50-90% of them will be extinct**.

That's not a typo. Between 3,500 and 6,400 languages — each one a unique way of understanding the world, encoding thousands of years of knowledge about local ecosystems, social structures, and human experience — will fall silent within our lifetime.

The math is brutal: if a language has fewer than 1,000 speakers, all of them elderly, it's functionally dead — even if the last speaker hasn't died yet.

## Why Languages Die

Languages don't die because they're "inferior." They die because of power.

**Colonization** — Hundreds of languages were actively suppressed. Australia had 250+ Aboriginal languages before colonization; today, only 13 are learned by children. The US and Canadian residential school systems explicitly forbade Indigenous languages.

**Economic Pressure** — If the dominant language is required for jobs, education, and government services, parents make the rational choice to raise children in that language. Each generation loses fluency.

**Digital Absence** — In 2026, approximately 7% of the world's languages have *any* digital presence. If you can't text, search, or post in your language, it becomes invisible in the modern world.

**Urbanization** — When young people move from rural communities to cities, they enter dominant-language environments. The village where grandparents speak the ancestral language empties out.

## Enter AI: The Preservation Machine

### Automatic Speech Recognition for Low-Resource Languages

The core problem: building AI for a language requires data. English has petabytes. Yoruba has gigabytes. Ainu has hours.

Recent breakthroughs have dramatically changed the equation:

**Meta's MMS (Massively Multilingual Speech)** — Trained on 1,100+ languages, it can perform speech recognition on languages it's barely seen. The model uses cross-lingual transfer: patterns learned from data-rich languages help bootstrap understanding of data-poor ones.

**Google's USM (Universal Speech Model)** — Trained on 12 million hours of audio across 300+ languages. For the first time, a single model can transcribe speech in languages that have zero written tradition.

**OpenAI's Whisper** — While focused on major languages, its architecture has been fine-tuned by researchers for endangered languages with as little as **200 hours of audio**.

### Text Generation and Translation

**NLLB (No Language Left Behind)** — Meta's translation model covers 200 languages, including many with very limited training data. It enables speakers of endangered languages to communicate across language barriers without switching to a dominant language.

But translation has limits. When you translate Pirahã (an Amazonian language with no number words, no color terms, and no perfect tense) into English, you've already lost something essential.

### Documentation

AI's most uncontroversial role: helping linguists document languages faster.

**ELAN + AI** — The standard linguistic annotation tool now integrates AI-assisted transcription and glossing. What took a linguist 100 hours to transcribe manually can be draft-transcribed by AI in minutes, then corrected by the linguist.

**SayMore** — Software for community-based language documentation that uses AI to help native speakers create annotated recordings of their language.

**Language Archive tools** — AI helps organize, tag, and make searchable the growing archives of recorded languages. The Endangered Languages Archive (ELAR) holds recordings of 700+ languages, now searchable by AI-powered phonetic matching.

## The Paradox of AI Preservation

Here's the uncomfortable question: **Is a language preserved by AI still alive?**

### The Museum Problem

A language recorded, transcribed, and archived is like a butterfly pinned in a display case — technically preserved, but no longer flying.

Language is not just grammar and vocabulary. It's:
- **Pragmatics** — The social rules of when to speak, how to show respect, when to be indirect
- **Embodiment** — Gestures, facial expressions, and body language that are inseparable from the words
- **Context** — The physical environment that gives meaning (a language born in a rainforest carries the rainforest in its words)
- **Evolution** — Living languages change. Preserved languages freeze.

### The Revitalization Approach

Some projects go beyond preservation to **revitalization** — actually bringing languages back into daily use:

**Duolingo** now offers courses in Hawaiian, Navajo, and Scottish Gaelic, reaching millions of potential learners. Hawaiian speakers increased from ~2,000 in 1990 to ~24,000 in 2025, partly driven by digital learning tools.

**Te Hiku Media** (New Zealand Māori) built their own AI speech recognition system specifically for te reo Māori, insisting on **Indigenous data sovereignty** — the data stays under Māori control, not in Silicon Valley servers.

**First Voices** — A platform by Indigenous communities in Canada to teach their languages through interactive, community-controlled digital tools.

## Who Owns a Language's Data?

This is becoming a fierce debate.

When a tech company trains an AI model on recordings of an endangered language, who owns the resulting model?

**The community's position:** The language belongs to us. Any AI trained on our language should be controlled by us, benefit us, and require our consent.

**The tech company's position:** Models trained on publicly available data are our intellectual property. We're helping preserve the language.

**The researcher's position:** Language data collected for academic purposes shouldn't be commercialized without community consent.

**Te Hiku Media's precedent** is important: they built the Māori speech recognition model themselves and established a license that prevents commercial use without Māori permission. They explicitly rejected Google's offer to "help" — because helping meant taking the data.

## The Languages AI Can't Reach

Some languages resist digital preservation:

**Oral-only languages** — Approximately 40% of the world's languages have no writing system. AI can process speech, but the lack of text makes traditional NLP approaches difficult.

**Tonal languages with micro-variation** — Some languages have tonal distinctions so subtle that current AI struggles to capture them. Hmong has 8 tones; some varieties of Vietnamese have 6. Yoruba's tone system carries meaning that AI models frequently misinterpret.

**Sign languages** — There are 300+ sign languages worldwide, many endangered. AI sign language recognition is improving but remains far behind speech recognition.

**Whistled languages** — Silbo Gomero (Canary Islands), sfyria (Greece), and several others encode full linguistic information in whistled tones. These languages are exceptionally difficult for AI to process.

## What's Being Lost

When a language dies, we don't just lose words. We lose:

**Ecological Knowledge** — Indigenous languages in the Amazon contain detailed botanical classifications that Western science is still discovering. The Hanunó'o people of the Philippines distinguish 1,600+ plant types — knowledge encoded in their language.

**Mathematical Concepts** — The Pirahã language challenges fundamental assumptions about human cognition (no recursion, no numbers, no creation myths). Its existence reshaped linguistic theory.

**Medical Knowledge** — Traditional medicine vocabulary in endangered languages often describes plant combinations and preparation methods that pharmaceutical companies would pay millions to access.

**Philosophical Frameworks** — The Hopi language conceptualizes time differently than Indo-European languages (arguably without past/future tense). This isn't just trivia — it represents a fundamentally different way of experiencing existence.

## Korea's Linguistic Heritage

South Korea faces its own language preservation challenges:

**Jeju Language (제주어)** — UNESCO classifies it as critically endangered. Approximately 5,000-10,000 fluent speakers remain, mostly over age 70. The Jeju language has unique vocabulary (especially for weather, ocean, and agriculture) absent from standard Korean.

**NIKL (국립국어원)** has accelerated digital documentation, but AI tools specifically for Jeju language processing are still in early stages.

**North Korean dialect divergence** — After 80+ years of separation, North and South Korean have diverged significantly. AI translation between the two varieties is an active research area, important for potential future reunification.

## The Realistic Path Forward

1. **Community-First AI** — Technology built *by* and *for* language communities, not imposed from outside. The most successful projects are community-led.

2. **Digital Presence** — Keyboards, fonts, social media support, and website localization. If you can't use your language on your phone, it's losing ground every day.

3. **Education Integration** — AI-powered language learning tools integrated into schools where endangered languages are spoken.

4. **Economic Incentive** — Creating economic value in endangered languages (AI translation services, cultural tourism, digital content) so speaking the language becomes an asset, not a liability.

5. **Archival Insurance** — Even if revitalization fails, comprehensive documentation ensures the language can be studied, understood, and potentially revived in the future.

---

## The Bottom Line

AI can't save a language by itself. Languages live in communities, in daily conversation, in the mouths of children learning their first words.

But AI can buy time. It can make documentation faster, learning more accessible, and digital presence possible. It can bridge the gap between endangered languages and the digital world that threatens them.

The question isn't whether AI is the perfect solution. The question is whether we'll deploy it fast enough — because every two weeks, the clock runs out on another language that took millennia to evolve.
